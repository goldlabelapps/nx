# English for Software Developers

In today's global software industry, English proficiency is as crucial as technical skills. Whether you're contributing to open-source projects, working in international teams, or advancing your career, strong English communication can be your competitive advantage.

## Why English Matters for Developers

### Global Collaboration is the Standard
- 90% of tech documentation is in English
- International teams span multiple time zones and cultures
- Open-source contribution requires clear written communication
- Remote work opportunities often require English proficiency

### Career Advancement Requires Communication
Technical skills get you hired; communication skills get you promoted. Senior roles involve presenting ideas, mentoring juniors, and representing your team to stakeholders.

## Technical English vs. General English

Software development has its own vocabulary, idioms, and communication patterns that differ from general English learning:

### Developer-Specific Language
- Technical vocabulary (deploy, refactor, optimise)
- Code review terminology
- Agile and Scrum vocabulary
- Architecture discussion frameworks

### Practical Communication Contexts
- Writing clear commit messages and PR descriptions
- Explaining technical concepts to non-technical stakeholders
- Participating in stand-ups and sprint planning
- Documenting APIs and code

## Common Challenges for Non-Native English Speakers

### Code Review Communication
Writing constructive feedback without sounding harsh is challenging even for native speakers. Learning diplomatic phrasing and technical precision is essential.

### Real-Time Meetings
Thinking in your native language while speaking in English creates delays. Video calls add accent comprehension and audio quality challenges.

### Written Documentation
Technical writing requires clarity, conciseness, and structure—different from the academic English taught in most courses.

### Imposter Syndrome
Language barriers amplify imposter syndrome, making developers hesitant to share ideas or ask questions.
